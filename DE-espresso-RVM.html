Teachers needed a way to go back to previously seen content.

Background

About product

espresso is a product to help teachers plan their lessons offering resources based on the UK curriculum.

The team

A cross-functional team with 2 backend and frontend developers, a product manager, product owner, a UX researcher and myself as product designer.

Users

The main users of espresso were teachers, they used the platform to plan lessons and present exercises in front of the class. They weren't tech savvy and had not much time to plan their lessons.

The problem

User research made us aware that teachers felt frustated to not find some resources they had been looking at the days before. They were using word documents to paste links and saving to favourites in the browser to keep track.

Exploration of solutions

Team workshop

The whole team gathered to think about solutions that would solve that problem

Possible solutions - pros/cons

  • A save to favourites option - This option would have been great but there were technical constrains making it almost impossible to achive.
  • A history page accessed from the main menu - A section on the homepage won as if our main objective was to make the user's life easier having to find the solution on the already overcrowded menu wouldn't be an option
  • A section on the homepage - This was the most technically viable and user-friendly solution as it will be highly visible.

Kicking off

Design goals and requirements

  • An unintrusive way of displaying the most recently viewed modules
  • Be able to recognise the module from the card
  • Show modules from most recent
  • Easy to navigate

Design

Position

Based on data

Based on qualitative and quantitative data we knew that the top visible part of the homescreen, around the main menu, is where the users spent more time in and used to navigate the platform so we thought there would be the best place to add the new section.

Functionality

Options

We thought about a static section with a show more button to lead them to another page or a carousell where they could navigate throughout the history without leaving the page.

Why we chose carousell

Because the homepage and its sections were a main point to discover content relevant to everyday planning, we decided to give the full functionality in the same place.

Designing the section

Help from styleguide

I moved between paper sketches directly to designing on a high fidelity wireframe as I had components from the styleguide and was very easy to put together and see the final look right away.

Cards design

Options

The modules needed an image and text that would be recognisable. We started exploring options that would do that and would't take much time to create.

The chosen ones

We choose a mix of using images from the module and text with the title of the module underneath so the text would be easy and quick to read and the image would help recognise the module.

Evaluative research

Testing

We wanted to find out if teachers would find the new section on the platform, if they would interact with the carrousel and overall if it was bringing them value.

We tested internally with the teachers that were working in-house.

We worked on the UI at the same time of development, because of that we moved quickly and we were able to test directly on the platform.

Main learnings

We had two arrows and users would click on both even if one was looking disabled so we removed the right or left arrow depending if the carousel was at the end of the list or at the beginning.

Besides that, some visuals on the cards that were a bit confusing but overall it worked very well.

After this round of testing we launched the feature and we kept learning about its use throught quantitative data, using Hot Jar Analytics.

Final UI

This feature helps teachers plan their lessons more efficiently.

Learnings

On what went well

Used qualitative and quantitative data to base the development of a feature on an actual problem the users were having.

To be improved

We could have validated the solution with real users by doing focus groups and participatory design workshops instead of looking for a solution ourselves.

A/B testing on the cards visuals would have given us data to see which were performing better but our platform didn't allow such tests.
